Civil Engineering

Calculus and Differential Methods

Differentiation, integration, ordinary differential equations and numerical methods for civil engineering applications.

Professional learner notes

Purpose of the topic

Calculus handles change and accumulation: road gradients, flow accumulation, settlement rates, turning points, areas under curves and numerical solutions.

These notes are written as class notes for engineering trainees. They follow the same working standard used in class: state the formula, substitute correctly, compute, attach units, and then explain what the answer means in a civil, building or survey situation.

Section 1

Rates of change and derivative meaning

The derivative measures how one quantity changes with another. In civil work, it may represent gradient, velocity of flow, settlement rate or change in cost.

Key formulae

\[y=f(x)\quad\Rightarrow\quad \frac{dy}{dx}=f'(x)\]

Worked example

If y is road level and x is chainage, dy/dx is the slope of the road profile.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

A derivative turns a profile equation into a gradient function.

Section 2

Differentiation rules

The power rule differentiates polynomial terms. Product, quotient and chain rules handle more advanced functions.

Key formulae

\[\frac{d}{dx}(ax^n)=anx^{n-1}\]
\[\frac{d}{dx}(uv)=u\frac{dv}{dx}+v\frac{du}{dx}\]

Worked example

Differentiate y=5x³-4x²+7x-9. dy/dx=15x²-8x+7.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used to find gradients and rates of change in engineering models.

Section 3

Applications of differentiation

Stationary points occur where dy/dx=0. They are used in optimization and curve analysis.

Key formulae

\[\frac{dy}{dx}=0\]

Worked example

For y=x²-6x+10, dy/dx=2x-6. Setting 2x-6=0 gives x=3.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

This helps identify minimum or maximum levels in modelled curves.

Section 4

Integration and area

Integration reverses differentiation and finds accumulated quantities such as area, volume, total flow or total change.

Key formulae

\[\int ax^n\,dx=\frac{ax^{n+1}}{n+1}+C\]
\[A=\int_a^b y\,dx\]

Worked example

∫₀⁴(2x+1)dx=[x²+x]₀⁴=20 square units.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Area under a curve can represent section area or accumulated flow.

Section 5

ODEs and numerical methods

Differential equations model systems involving rates. Numerical methods estimate solutions when exact methods are difficult.

Key formulae

\[\frac{dy}{dx}+P(x)y=Q(x)\]
\[x_{n+1}=x_n-\frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}\]

Worked example

For f(x)=x³-4x-9 and x₀=2.5, Newton-Raphson gives x₁=x₀-f(x₀)/f'(x₀).

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Numerical methods support root-finding in design equations.

Practice exercises

  1. Differentiate y=4x³-3x²+2x-1.
  2. Find the gradient of y=0.02x²+0.5x+100 at x=30.
  3. Integrate 6x²-4x+3.
  4. Evaluate ∫₀³ x² dx.
  5. Use one Newton-Raphson iteration for f(x)=x²-5 from x₀=2.

Summary

By the end of this topic, a competent trainee should be able to identify the correct formula, perform the calculation accurately, use correct units, and explain the result in a real engineering context.
