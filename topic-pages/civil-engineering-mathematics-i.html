Engineering Mathematics I: Mathematical Principles

Foundation mathematics for civil engineering trainees: arithmetic, algebra, mensuration, trigonometry, statistics and linear graphs.

Purpose of the topic

Engineering Mathematics I gives civil trainees the calculation foundation used in site measurement, concrete quantities, drainage sections, road gradients, statistical records and technical graphs.

These notes are written as class notes for engineering trainees. They follow the same working standard used in class: state the formula, substitute correctly, compute, attach units, and then explain what the answer means in a civil, building or survey situation.

Section 1

Number work, fractions, decimals and percentages

Civil work requires accurate quantity calculation. Fractions appear in ratios, decimals appear in measurements, and percentages appear in wastage, slope and material variation.

Key formulae

\[\mathrm{Percentage}=\frac{\mathrm{part}}{\mathrm{whole}}\times100\%\]
\[\mathrm{Ratio}=a:b\]

Worked example

A concrete batch uses a 1:2:4 mix. Total dry material is 700 kg. Total parts = 7, so cement = 100 kg, sand = 200 kg and aggregate = 400 kg.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

This is used when estimating small concrete mixes before ordering materials.

Section 2

Algebra and formula substitution

Algebra allows a technician to work with formulas where one value is unknown. Letters may represent length, area, volume, cost, force, gradient or time.

Key formulae

\[A=lw\]
\[V=Ah\]
\[w=\frac{A}{l}\]

Worked example

If a rectangular slab has area 48 m² and length 8 m, then width is w=A/l=48/8=6 m.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

This supports checking missing dimensions from drawings or site measurements.

Section 3

Mensuration for civil works

Mensuration deals with area and volume. Civil trainees use it for earthworks, concrete, drainage, road sections and material estimates.

Key formulae

\[A=\frac{1}{2}(a+b)h\]
\[V=lwh\]
\[V=Ah\]

Worked example

A trapezoidal drain has parallel sides 0.8 m and 1.4 m, depth 0.6 m. A=1/2(0.8+1.4)(0.6)=0.66 m².

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

The cross-sectional area helps estimate excavation or flow capacity.

Section 4

Trigonometry and gradients

Trigonometry connects sides and angles. It supports slopes, heights, setting out and right-triangle calculations.

Key formulae

\[\tan\theta=\frac{\mathrm{rise}}{\mathrm{run}}\]
\[a^2+b^2=c^2\]

Worked example

A road rises 2.4 m over 80 m. Gradient = 2.4/80 = 0.03 = 3%.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Gradient is used to judge road slope and drainage behaviour.

Section 5

Statistics and linear graphs

Civil technicians record test results such as cube strengths, CBR values and site observations. Graphs show trends and relationships.

Key formulae

\[\bar{x}=\frac{\sum x}{n}\]
\[m=\frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}\]
\[y=mx+c\]

Worked example

Cube strengths 24, 26, 25, 27 and 23 N/mm² have mean = 125/5 = 25 N/mm².

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Mean values help summarize material quality before making judgement.

Practice exercises

  1. Find the concrete quantities for a 1:3:6 mix using 900 kg total material.
  2. A road rises 4 m over 160 m. Find the gradient as a percentage.
  3. Find the area of a trapezium with parallel sides 1.2 m and 2.0 m and depth 0.75 m.
  4. Find the mean of 18, 21, 20, 19 and 22.
  5. Given y=0.05x+100, find y when x=60.

Summary

By the end of this topic, a competent trainee should be able to identify the correct formula, perform the calculation accurately, use correct units, and explain the result in a real engineering context.
