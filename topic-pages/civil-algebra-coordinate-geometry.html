Civil Engineering

Apply Algebra and Coordinate Geometry

Indices, logarithms, equations, simultaneous equations, coordinate geometry and binomial expansion for civil engineering layouts.

Professional learner notes

Purpose of the topic

This unit strengthens algebraic manipulation and coordinate reasoning used in road alignments, setting out, profile lines, control points and structural calculations.

These notes are written as class notes for engineering trainees. They follow the same working standard used in class: state the formula, substitute correctly, compute, attach units, and then explain what the answer means in a civil, building or survey situation.

Section 1

Indices and engineering notation

Indices simplify repeated multiplication and support standard form calculations in engineering measurements.

Key formulae

\[a^m a^n=a^{m+n}\]
\[\frac{a^m}{a^n}=a^{m-n}\]
\[(a^m)^n=a^{mn}\]

Worked example

x³ × x² = x⁵. This follows because the powers have the same base.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Index laws are useful in scientific notation, areas, volumes and engineering data.

Section 2

Logarithms and laws

Logarithms are used to solve exponential relationships and transform nonlinear laws into straight-line form.

Key formulae

\[\log(ab)=\log a+\log b\]
\[\log\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)=\log a-\log b\]
\[\log(a^n)=n\log a\]

Worked example

If y=axⁿ, then log y = log a + n log x. Plotting log y against log x gives a straight line.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

This helps verify experimental laws from material or hydraulic data.

Section 3

Linear and simultaneous equations

Civil problems often contain two unknown quantities. Simultaneous equations model paired conditions.

Key formulae

\[a_1x+b_1y=c_1\]
\[a_2x+b_2y=c_2\]

Worked example

If x+y=30 and 800x+300y=14000, then y=30-x and x=10, y=20.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

This can represent material quantities, cost equations or labour allocation.

Section 4

Coordinate geometry

Coordinate geometry connects algebra with layout. Eastings and northings are treated like x and y coordinates.

Key formulae

\[d=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}\]
\[m=\frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}\]
\[y-y_1=m(x-x_1)\]

Worked example

For A(120,250) and B(180,330), d=sqrt(60²+80²)=100 m.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

This checks distance between control points or setting-out stations.

Section 5

Binomial expansion

The binomial theorem expands powers of two-term expressions and estimates small changes.

Key formulae

\[(a+b)^n=a^n+na^{n-1}b+\frac{n(n-1)}{2!}a^{n-2}b^2+\cdots\]

Worked example

(1+x)³ = 1+3x+3x²+x³.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

This is useful in approximation and mathematical modelling in engineering.

Practice exercises

  1. Simplify x⁵/x².
  2. Solve 2x+3=15.
  3. Find the distance between A(40,75) and B(100,155).
  4. Find the gradient of the line through (2,5) and (8,17).
  5. Expand (1+2x)^4 up to the x² term.

Summary

By the end of this topic, a competent trainee should be able to identify the correct formula, perform the calculation accurately, use correct units, and explain the result in a real engineering context.
