Land Survey

Engineering Mathematics II for Land Survey

Coordinate geometry, bearings, gradients, latitudes, departures and area from coordinates.

Purpose of the topic

Engineering Mathematics II develops the coordinate and traverse computations used in land survey work.

These notes are written as class notes for engineering trainees. They follow the same working standard used in class: state the formula, substitute correctly, compute, attach units, and then explain what the answer means in a civil, building or survey situation.

Section 1

Coordinate geometry

Coordinates allow distances, gradients and line equations to be calculated.

Key formulae

\[d=\sqrt{(\Delta E)^2+(\Delta N)^2}\]
\[m=\frac{\Delta N}{\Delta E}\]

Worked example

If ΔE=40 and ΔN=30, distance=50 m and slope relative to east is 30/40=0.75.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used in plotting and checking control lines.

Section 2

Whole-circle bearings

Whole-circle bearings are measured clockwise from north from 0° to 360°.

Key formulae

\[\theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{|\Delta E|}{|\Delta N|}\right)\]

Worked example

Quadrant corrections depend on signs of ΔE and ΔN.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Essential for correct traverse direction.

Section 3

Latitudes and departures

Latitude is north-south component; departure is east-west component.

Key formulae

\[\Delta E=L\sin\theta\]
\[\Delta N=L\cos\theta\]

Worked example

For L=100 m and θ=35°, ΔE=57.36 m and ΔN=81.92 m.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used in traverse computation.

Section 4

Gradients and slopes

Gradient compares rise to horizontal run.

Key formulae

\[\mathrm{Gradient}=\frac{\mathrm{Rise}}{\mathrm{Run}}\]

Worked example

Rise 1.5 m over 60 m gives gradient=1/40 or 2.5%.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used in levelling and engineering survey.

Section 5

Area from coordinates

Coordinate area uses ordered boundary coordinates.

Key formulae

\[A=\frac12\left|\sum x_iy_{i+1}-\sum y_ix_{i+1}\right|\]

Worked example

Tabulate coordinates clockwise, multiply cross-products and take half the absolute difference.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used in parcel-area computation.

Practice exercises

  1. Compute latitude and departure for L=80 m, bearing 40°.
  2. Find gradient for rise 2 m over 100 m.
  3. Explain whole-circle bearing correction in quadrant III.
  4. Find distance from ΔE=24, ΔN=32.
  5. Set up a coordinate-area table for four points.

Summary

By the end of this topic, a competent trainee should be able to identify the correct formula, perform the calculation accurately, use correct units, and explain the result in a real engineering context.
