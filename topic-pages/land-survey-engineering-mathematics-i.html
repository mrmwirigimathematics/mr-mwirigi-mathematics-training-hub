Engineering Mathematics I for Land Survey

Arithmetic, coordinates, bearings, trigonometry and distance calculations for survey fieldwork.

Purpose of the topic

Engineering Mathematics I gives land survey trainees the foundation for field measurements, coordinates, bearings and right-triangle calculations.

These notes are written as class notes for engineering trainees. They follow the same working standard used in class: state the formula, substitute correctly, compute, attach units, and then explain what the answer means in a civil, building or survey situation.

Section 1

Survey arithmetic and units

Survey calculations require careful units, decimals and rounding.

Key formulae

\[1\ \mathrm{m}=1000\ \mathrm{mm}\]

Worked example

A tape reading of 12.375 m is 12375 mm.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Accurate unit conversion prevents field-book errors.

Section 2

Coordinates and differences

Survey points are commonly described by eastings and northings.

Key formulae

\[\Delta E=E_2-E_1\]
\[\Delta N=N_2-N_1\]

Worked example

If E changes from 200 to 260 and N from 100 to 180, ΔE=60 and ΔN=80.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Coordinate differences are used to calculate distance and bearing.

Section 3

Distance from coordinates

Pythagoras gives distance between two coordinate points.

Key formulae

\[d=\sqrt{(\Delta E)^2+(\Delta N)^2}\]

Worked example

For ΔE=60 and ΔN=80, d=sqrt(60²+80²)=100 m.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used to check survey line lengths.

Section 4

Bearings

Bearing is direction measured clockwise from north.

Key formulae

\[\theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\Delta E}{\Delta N}\right)\]

Worked example

For ΔE=60 and ΔN=80, θ=tan⁻¹(60/80)=36.87°.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

This is a north-east line bearing.

Section 5

Trigonometry for heighting

Angles and distances can determine inaccessible heights.

Key formulae

\[\tan\theta=\frac{\mathrm{height}}{\mathrm{horizontal\ distance}}\]

Worked example

Height = 20 tan 25° = 9.33 m.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used where direct height measurement is not practical.

Practice exercises

  1. Convert 2.375 km to metres.
  2. Find ΔE and ΔN between two points.
  3. Find distance for ΔE=45 m and ΔN=60 m.
  4. Find bearing angle for ΔE=30 and ΔN=70.
  5. Estimate height from 15 m distance and 32° angle.

Summary

By the end of this topic, a competent trainee should be able to identify the correct formula, perform the calculation accurately, use correct units, and explain the result in a real engineering context.
