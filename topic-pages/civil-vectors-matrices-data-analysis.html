Civil Engineering

Vectors, Matrices and Data Analysis

Vectors, matrix methods, simultaneous equations, statistics and engineering data interpretation.

Lesson menu

Professional learner notes

Purpose of the topic

This unit supports force resolution, displacement, material data analysis, matrix solving and technical decision-making from data.

These notes are written as class notes for engineering trainees. They follow the same working standard used in class: state the formula, substitute correctly, compute, attach units, and then explain what the answer means in a civil, building or survey situation.

Section 1

Scalars and vectors

Scalars have magnitude only; vectors have magnitude and direction. Force, displacement and velocity are vectors.

Key formulae

\[\vec{R}=\vec{A}+\vec{B}\]

Worked example

Two forces acting in the same direction add directly. If 5 kN and 7 kN act together, resultant=12 kN.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Vector thinking is needed in structural and survey calculations.

Section 2

Resolution of vectors

A vector can be split into horizontal and vertical components.

Key formulae

\[A_x=A\cos\theta\]
\[A_y=A\sin\theta\]

Worked example

A 12 kN force at 35° has Aₓ=12cos35°=9.83 kN and Aᵧ=12sin35°=6.88 kN.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Components help analyse forces in members.

Section 3

Matrices and systems

Matrices organise simultaneous equations and transformations. The system AX=B represents several equations compactly.

Key formulae

\[AX=B\]
\[\det(A)=ad-bc\]

Worked example

Equations 2x+y=8 and x+3y=9 can be written as [[2,1],[1,3]][x,y]^T=[8,9]^T.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Matrix notation supports efficient calculation in engineering systems.

Section 4

Statistics for engineering data

Engineering measurements vary. Statistics summarise centre and spread.

Key formulae

\[\bar{x}=\frac{\sum x}{n}\]
\[s=\sqrt{\frac{\sum(x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1}}\]

Worked example

Mean concrete strength is found by adding all results and dividing by the number tested.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Standard deviation shows consistency of results.

Section 5

Probability and quality checks

Probability helps estimate likelihood and support quality decisions.

Key formulae

\[P(A)=\frac{n(A)}{n(S)}\]

Worked example

If 3 out of 20 samples fail, probability of failure from the sample is 3/20=0.15.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

This supports risk and quality discussions.

Practice exercises

  1. Resolve a 20 kN force at 30° into components.
  2. Write two simultaneous equations in matrix form.
  3. Calculate the mean of 22,25,23,24,26.
  4. Find probability if 4 out of 25 readings are outside tolerance.
  5. Explain why standard deviation matters in concrete testing.

Summary

By the end of this topic, a competent trainee should be able to identify the correct formula, perform the calculation accurately, use correct units, and explain the result in a real engineering context.
