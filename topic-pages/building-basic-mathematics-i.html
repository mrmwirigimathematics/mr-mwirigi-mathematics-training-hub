Basic Mathematics I for Building Technology

Arithmetic, fractions, ratios, percentages, algebra and basic statistics for building trainees.

Purpose of the topic

Basic Mathematics I supports masonry, site measurement, material ordering, wastage estimates and basic building calculations.

These notes are written as class notes for engineering trainees. They follow the same working standard used in class: state the formula, substitute correctly, compute, attach units, and then explain what the answer means in a civil, building or survey situation.

Section 1

Fractions, decimals and percentages

Building quantities often involve fractional dimensions, decimal measurements and percentage wastage.

Key formulae

\[\mathrm{Percentage}=\frac{\mathrm{part}}{\mathrm{whole}}\times100\%\]

Worked example

Tiles required = 120 m² plus 8% wastage = 120(1.08)=129.6 m².

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

This prevents under-ordering materials.

Section 2

Ratios in mortar and concrete

Ratios describe how materials are mixed. A 1:4 mortar mix has 1 cement part and 4 sand parts.

Key formulae

\[\mathrm{Total\ parts}=1+4=5\]

Worked example

If cement is 50 kg in a 1:4 mix, sand is 4×50=200 kg.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used for mortar batching.

Section 3

Basic algebra

Algebra solves missing dimensions and cost equations.

Key formulae

\[A=lw\]
\[l=\frac{A}{w}\]

Worked example

If area is 36 m² and width is 4 m, length=36/4=9 m.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used in reading building plans.

Section 4

Mensuration

Mensuration gives perimeters, areas and volumes.

Key formulae

\[P=2(l+w)\]
\[A=lw\]
\[V=lwh\]

Worked example

A room 5 m by 4 m has floor area 20 m² and perimeter 18 m.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Supports flooring, skirting and painting estimates.

Section 5

Basic statistics

Statistics summarises production records and site measurements.

Key formulae

\[\bar{x}=\frac{\sum x}{n}\]

Worked example

Daily block outputs 400,420,390,410 have mean 405 blocks.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used for productivity monitoring.

Practice exercises

  1. Add 10% wastage to 75 m² of tiles.
  2. Find sand required for 60 kg cement in a 1:5 mix.
  3. Find perimeter of a 6 m by 4.5 m room.
  4. Calculate volume of a 3 m by 2 m by 0.2 m slab.
  5. Find the mean of five daily outputs.

Summary

By the end of this topic, a competent trainee should be able to identify the correct formula, perform the calculation accurately, use correct units, and explain the result in a real engineering context.
