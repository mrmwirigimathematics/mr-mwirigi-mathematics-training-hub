Building Technology · Basic Mathematics

Professional Notes on Mensuration, Arithmetic, Algebra and Statistics

These notes support building students in solving practical site problems involving measurement, quantities, rates, proportions, material estimates, simple equations and interpretation of site data.

Learner notes

Topic Overview

Mathematics in building technology is not studied as isolated theory. It is used to estimate quantities, check dimensions, reduce wastage, prepare material schedules, interpret drawings and monitor site performance.

Learning Outcome 1: Carry out basic mensuration Units of measurement, unit conversion, perimeters, areas, volumes, surface areas and irregular figures.
Learning Outcome 2: Apply basic arithmetic Whole numbers, fractions, decimals, percentages, place value, ranges, rounding off, proportions and standard form.
Learning Outcome 3: Apply basic algebra Indices, logarithms, linear equations, calculator use, simultaneous equations and simple algebraic formulae.
Learning Outcome 4: Apply basic statistics Grouped and ungrouped data, frequency tables, mean, median, mode, charts and chart interpretation.

Professional working rule: always write the formula, substitute values with units, simplify carefully, state the final answer with correct units, then interpret the answer in a building context.

Learning Outcome 1

1. Carry Out Basic Mensuration

Mensuration deals with measurement of length, perimeter, area, volume and surface area. In building work, it is used when estimating flooring, walling, plastering, painting, concrete, excavation and roofing quantities.

1.1 Units of Measurement

Quantity Common Units Building Application
Length mm, cm, m, km Wall length, beam span, room dimensions, foundation trench length
Area mm², cm², m² Floor area, plastering area, painting area, tiling area
Volume cm³, m³, litres Concrete volume, excavation volume, water tank capacity
Mass g, kg, tonnes Cement, steel, aggregate, sand and material loads

1.2 Conversion of Units

Before calculating, all measurements must be expressed in compatible units. Many site errors occur when millimetres and metres are mixed without conversion.

Important Conversions

\[1\text{ m}=100\text{ cm}=1000\text{ mm}\]
\[1\text{ m}^2=10\,000\text{ cm}^2\]
\[1\text{ m}^3=1\,000\,000\text{ cm}^3\]
\[1\text{ m}^3=1000\text{ litres}\]

Worked Example 1: Unit Conversion for a Slab

A concrete slab is $150\text{ mm}$ thick. Convert the thickness to metres.

  1. Since $1000\text{ mm}=1\text{ m}$, divide by $1000$.
  2. \[150\text{ mm}=\frac{150}{1000}=0.15\text{ m}\]
  3. Answer: The slab thickness is $0.15\text{ m}$.

1.3 Perimeters and Areas

Perimeter is the distance around a shape. Area is the amount of surface covered by a shape. Perimeter is useful for skirting, fencing and edging. Area is useful for flooring, plastering, painting and roofing.

Perimeter and Area Formulae

Rectangle: \[P=2(l+w),\qquad A=lw\]
Square: \[P=4s,\qquad A=s^2\]
Triangle: \[A=\frac{1}{2}bh\]
Trapezium: \[A=\frac{1}{2}(a+b)h\]
Circle: \[C=2\pi r,\qquad A=\pi r^2\]
Composite Area: \[A_T=A_1+A_2+A_3+\cdots\]

Worked Example 2: Floor Area and Skirting Length

A room measures $5.4\text{ m}$ by $3.8\text{ m}$. Find the floor area and perimeter.

  1. Floor area: \[A=lw=5.4\times3.8=20.52\text{ m}^2\]
  2. Perimeter: \[P=2(l+w)=2(5.4+3.8)=18.4\text{ m}\]
  3. Interpretation: The floor finish covers $20.52\text{ m}^2$, while skirting requires approximately $18.4\text{ m}$ before allowing for openings and wastage.

1.4 Volume and Surface Area of Solids

Volume is used for concrete, excavation, backfilling and tank capacity. Surface area is used for painting, formwork, plastering and cladding.

Volume and Surface Area Formulae

Cuboid volume: \[V=lwh\]
Cube volume: \[V=s^3\]
Cylinder volume: \[V=\pi r^2h\]
Cylinder curved surface area: \[CSA=2\pi rh\]
Cuboid total surface area: \[TSA=2(lw+lh+wh)\]
Prism volume: \[V=\text{cross-sectional area}\times\text{length}\]

Worked Example 3: Concrete Volume for a Slab

A slab measures $6\text{ m}$ long, $4\text{ m}$ wide and $150\text{ mm}$ thick. Find the volume of concrete required.

  1. Convert thickness: \[150\text{ mm}=0.15\text{ m}\]
  2. Use volume formula: \[V=lwh\]
  3. Substitute: \[V=6\times4\times0.15=3.6\text{ m}^3\]
  4. Answer: Concrete required is $3.6\text{ m}^3$ before adding wastage.

1.5 Area of Irregular Figures

Irregular figures can be handled by splitting them into regular shapes or by using approximate methods such as the trapezoidal rule where offsets are taken at equal intervals.

Trapezoidal Rule for Irregular Area

\[ A=\frac{d}{2}\left[y_0+y_n+2(y_1+y_2+\cdots+y_{n-1})\right] \]

Where $d$ is the common interval and $y_0,y_1,\ldots,y_n$ are the measured offsets.

Worked Example 4: Irregular Plot Area

Offsets from a baseline at $5\text{ m}$ intervals are $2, 3, 4, 3, 2\text{ m}$. Estimate the area.

  1. Here $d=5$, $y_0=2$, $y_4=2$, and intermediate offsets are $3,4,3$.
  2. \[A=\frac{5}{2}[2+2+2(3+4+3)]\]
  3. \[A=2.5[4+20]=2.5\times24=60\text{ m}^2\]
  4. Answer: The estimated irregular area is $60\text{ m}^2$.
Learning Outcome 2

2. Apply Basic Arithmetic

Arithmetic is used in nearly every site calculation: counting materials, calculating costs, determining wastage, checking dimensions and estimating labour output.

2.1 Whole Numbers and Place Value

Whole numbers are numbers without fractions or decimal parts, such as $0,1,2,3,\ldots$. Place value shows the value of a digit depending on its position.

Number Expanded Form Building Meaning
4,725 $4000+700+20+5$ Could represent 4,725 blocks delivered to site
12,500 $10000+2000+500$ Could represent KSh 12,500 labour cost

2.2 Simple Fractions

A fraction represents part of a whole. In building, fractions appear in material proportions, partial lengths, drawings and scaling.

Fraction Operations

Addition: \[\frac{a}{b}+\frac{c}{b}=\frac{a+c}{b}\]
Multiplication: \[\frac{a}{b}\times\frac{c}{d}=\frac{ac}{bd}\]
Division: \[\frac{a}{b}\div\frac{c}{d}=\frac{a}{b}\times\frac{d}{c}\]
Fraction of quantity: \[\frac{a}{b}\text{ of }Q=\frac{a}{b}\times Q\]

Worked Example 5: Fraction of Cement Bags

A contractor uses $\frac{3}{4}$ of 20 bags of cement. How many bags are used?

  1. \[\frac{3}{4}\times20=15\]
  2. Answer: The contractor uses 15 bags.

2.3 Decimals

Decimals are common in measurements such as $2.75\text{ m}$, $0.15\text{ m}$ and $3.6\text{ m}^3$. They allow more accurate measurement than whole numbers.

2.4 Percentages

Percentage means per hundred. It is used for wastage, profit, discount, slope, moisture content and progress reports.

Percentage Formulae

\[\text{Percentage}=\frac{\text{part}}{\text{whole}}\times100\%\]
\[\text{Percentage increase}=\frac{\text{increase}}{\text{original}}\times100\%\]
\[\text{New amount}=\text{original}\times\left(1+\frac{r}{100}\right)\]
\[\text{Reduced amount}=\text{original}\times\left(1-\frac{r}{100}\right)\]

Worked Example 6: Tile Wastage

A room requires $48\text{ m}^2$ of tiles. Add 10% wastage.

  1. \[\text{Total}=48\times\left(1+\frac{10}{100}\right)\]
  2. \[\text{Total}=48\times1.10=52.8\text{ m}^2\]
  3. Answer: Order approximately $52.8\text{ m}^2$ of tiles.

2.5 Ranges

A range gives the difference between the highest and lowest value. It is useful for checking variation in block output, wall thickness measurements or daily labour production.

\[\text{Range}=\text{Highest value}-\text{Lowest value}\]

2.6 Rounding Off

Rounding gives a practical value suitable for ordering and reporting. In building estimates, quantities are often rounded upward to avoid shortage.

Professional caution

Do not round too early in multi-step calculations. Keep enough decimal places during working, then round the final answer.

2.7 Arithmetic Percentages and Proportions

Proportion compares quantities. It is very important in mortar, concrete, paint mixing and drawing scales.

Ratio and Proportion

\[a:b=\frac{a}{b}\]
\[\text{One part}=\frac{\text{Total quantity}}{\text{Total number of parts}}\]

Worked Example 7: Mortar Mix Ratio

A mortar mix is $1:4$ for cement:sand. If the total dry material is $0.50\text{ m}^3$, find cement and sand quantities.

  1. Total parts: \[1+4=5\]
  2. One part: \[\frac{0.50}{5}=0.10\text{ m}^3\]
  3. Cement: \[1\times0.10=0.10\text{ m}^3\]
  4. Sand: \[4\times0.10=0.40\text{ m}^3\]
  5. Answer: Cement is $0.10\text{ m}^3$ and sand is $0.40\text{ m}^3$.

2.8 Standard Form

Standard form writes very large or very small numbers compactly. It is useful in engineering calculations involving large quantities, densities or scientific measurements.

\[N=a\times10^n,\qquad 1\leq a<10\]

Worked Example 8: Writing a Number in Standard Form

Write $125000$ in standard form.

  1. Move the decimal point until one digit remains before it: $1.25$.
  2. The decimal point moves 5 places.
  3. \[125000=1.25\times10^5\]
Learning Outcome 3

3. Apply Basic Algebra

Algebra uses letters to represent unknown quantities. In building work, algebra helps solve missing dimensions, determine cost equations, rearrange formulae and solve simultaneous site constraints.

3.1 Indices and Logarithms

Indices are powers. They simplify repeated multiplication. Logarithms are the reverse operation of indices.

Laws of Indices

\[a^m\times a^n=a^{m+n}\]
\[\frac{a^m}{a^n}=a^{m-n}\]
\[(a^m)^n=a^{mn}\]
\[a^0=1,\qquad a\ne0\]
\[a^{-n}=\frac{1}{a^n}\]
\[a^{1/n}=\sqrt[n]{a}\]

Basic Logarithm Laws

\[\log(ab)=\log a+\log b\]
\[\log\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)=\log a-\log b\]
\[\log(a^n)=n\log a\]
\[\log_{10}(1000)=3\]

Worked Example 9: Applying Index Laws

Simplify $x^3\times x^4$.

  1. Use the multiplication law: \[a^m\times a^n=a^{m+n}\]
  2. \[x^3\times x^4=x^{3+4}=x^7\]
  3. Answer: $x^7$.

3.2 Linear Equations

A linear equation has the unknown raised to power one. The aim is to isolate the unknown.

\[ax+b=c\quad\Rightarrow\quad x=\frac{c-b}{a}\]

Worked Example 10: Labour Cost Equation

A mason charges a fixed transport fee of KSh 500 plus KSh 800 per day. The total cost is KSh 4,500. Find the number of days worked.

  1. Let $d$ be the number of days.
  2. Form equation: \[500+800d=4500\]
  3. Subtract 500: \[800d=4000\]
  4. Divide by 800: \[d=5\]
  5. Answer: The mason worked for 5 days.

3.3 Use of a Calculator to Solve Problems

A calculator improves speed, but it does not replace mathematical understanding. The learner must know the formula, the order of operations and the correct units before pressing calculator keys.

Calculator discipline

Use brackets for grouped operations. For example, calculate $2(5.4+3.8)$ as $2\times(5.4+3.8)$, not as $2\times5.4+3.8$.

3.4 Simultaneous Equations

Simultaneous equations are two or more equations solved together. They are useful when two unknown quantities are related.

\[ \begin{aligned} ax+by&=c\\ dx+ey&=f \end{aligned} \]

Worked Example 11: Finding Two Material Costs

Two bags of cement and one bag of lime cost KSh 1,700. One bag of cement and one bag of lime cost KSh 1,000. Find the cost of each material.

  1. Let cement cost be $c$ and lime cost be $l$.
  2. Form equations: \[2c+l=1700\] \[c+l=1000\]
  3. Subtract second equation from first: \[(2c+l)-(c+l)=1700-1000\]
  4. \[c=700\]
  5. Substitute into $c+l=1000$: \[700+l=1000\Rightarrow l=300\]
  6. Answer: Cement costs KSh 700 and lime costs KSh 300.

3.5 Simple Algebraic Equations and Formula Rearrangement

Formula rearrangement is important when the required quantity is not the subject of the formula.

Common Rearrangements

From \[A=lw\] get \[l=\frac{A}{w}\]
From \[V=lwh\] get \[h=\frac{V}{lw}\]
From \[P=2(l+w)\] get \[l=\frac{P}{2}-w\]
From \[C=2\pi r\] get \[r=\frac{C}{2\pi}\]

Worked Example 12: Finding Missing Width

A rectangular floor has area $42\text{ m}^2$ and length $7\text{ m}$. Find the width.

  1. Formula: \[A=lw\]
  2. Make $w$ the subject: \[w=\frac{A}{l}\]
  3. Substitute: \[w=\frac{42}{7}=6\text{ m}\]
  4. Answer: The width is $6\text{ m}$.
Learning Outcome 4

4. Apply Basic Statistics

Statistics helps building students organise, summarise and interpret site information such as material usage, labour output, test results, delivery quantities and defects.

4.1 Grouped and Ungrouped Data

Ungrouped Data

Raw individual values, for example daily block outputs:

\[420,\ 390,\ 410,\ 430,\ 400\]

Grouped Data

Values placed into classes, for example wall lengths:

\[0-5,\ 6-10,\ 11-15,\ 16-20\]

4.2 Ungrouped Data Organisation

Ungrouped data should be arranged in ascending or descending order before finding the median or checking spread.

Worked Example 13: Arranging Data

Arrange the following site delivery quantities in ascending order: $18, 12, 20, 15, 10$.

  1. Ascending order means smallest to largest.
  2. \[10,\ 12,\ 15,\ 18,\ 20\]

4.3 Frequency Table Data Representation

A frequency table shows how many times each value or class occurs.

Number of Defects per Wall Frequency Meaning
0 3 Three walls had no visible defects
1 5 Five walls had one visible defect
2 2 Two walls had two visible defects

4.4 Mean, Mode and Median

Measures of central tendency describe the typical value in a data set.

Ungrouped Data Formulae

Mean: \[\bar{x}=\frac{\sum x}{n}\]
Median: \[\text{middle value after arranging data}\]
Mode: \[\text{most repeated value}\]
Range: \[R=x_{\max}-x_{\min}\]

Worked Example 14: Mean, Median and Mode

Daily block outputs are $380, 400, 420, 400, 450$ blocks. Find the mean, median and mode.

  1. Mean: \[\bar{x}=\frac{380+400+420+400+450}{5}=\frac{2050}{5}=410\]
  2. Arrange data: \[380,\ 400,\ 400,\ 420,\ 450\]
  3. Median is the middle value: \[400\]
  4. Mode is the most repeated value: \[400\]
  5. Answer: Mean = 410 blocks, Median = 400 blocks, Mode = 400 blocks.

Grouped Mean

For grouped data, use class midpoints. The midpoint is found by adding the lower and upper class limits and dividing by two.

\[\bar{x}=\frac{\sum fx}{\sum f}\]

Where $f$ is frequency and $x$ is the class midpoint.

Worked Example 15: Grouped Mean

The table shows the number of blocks laid by workers in one day.

Blocks Laid Frequency $f$ Midpoint $x$ $fx$
100 - 199 2 149.5 299
200 - 299 5 249.5 1247.5
300 - 399 3 349.5 1048.5
  1. \[\sum f=2+5+3=10\]
  2. \[\sum fx=299+1247.5+1048.5=2595\]
  3. \[\bar{x}=\frac{2595}{10}=259.5\]
  4. Answer: The estimated mean output is $259.5$ blocks per worker.

4.5 Charts

Charts present data visually. In building, charts may show daily output, cost changes, defect frequency, material usage or project progress.

Chart Type Best Used For Building Example
Bar chart Comparing separate categories Comparing output of different workers
Line graph Showing change over time Tracking weekly material cost
Pie chart Showing parts of a whole Showing percentage distribution of project costs
Histogram Showing grouped continuous data Showing distribution of concrete strength test results

4.6 Chart Data Interpretation

When interpreting a chart, identify the title, axes, units, scale, highest value, lowest value, trend and practical meaning.

Chart interpretation checklist

  1. What does the chart represent?
  2. What units are used?
  3. Which value is highest and which is lowest?
  4. Is the pattern increasing, decreasing or fluctuating?
  5. What site decision can be made from the data?

Practice Questions for Building Students

  1. Convert $225\text{ mm}$ into metres.
  2. A room measures $4.8\text{ m}$ by $3.6\text{ m}$. Find its floor area.
  3. Find the perimeter of a rectangular room measuring $7\text{ m}$ by $5\text{ m}$.
  4. A slab measures $8\text{ m}$ by $5\text{ m}$ by $0.125\text{ m}$. Find its concrete volume.
  5. A tiling job needs $62\text{ m}^2$ of tiles. Add 8% wastage.
  6. Simplify $a^5\times a^2$.
  7. Solve $3x+7=31$.
  8. Solve simultaneously: $x+y=12$ and $2x+y=19$.
  9. Find the mean of $12,15,18,20,25$.
  10. State two advantages of using charts to represent building site data.
Competency checklist

Summary Checklist

A competent learner can:

  • Convert units correctly before calculation.
  • Find perimeter, area, volume and surface area.
  • Estimate area of irregular figures.
  • Use fractions, decimals, percentages and proportions.
  • Solve simple and simultaneous algebraic equations.
  • Organise data using frequency tables.
  • Calculate mean, median and mode.
  • Interpret charts in a building context.

Common mistakes to avoid:

  • Using millimetres and metres in the same formula without conversion.
  • Forgetting square units for area and cubic units for volume.
  • Rounding too early during calculation.
  • Writing answers without units.
  • Using a calculator without brackets for grouped operations.
  • Interpreting charts without checking axes and scale.
