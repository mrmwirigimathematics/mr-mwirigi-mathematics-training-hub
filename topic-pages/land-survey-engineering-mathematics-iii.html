Topic Overview

Engineering Mathematics III for Land Survey supports the treatment of survey observations as numerical evidence. Surveyors collect repeated distance, bearing, level, coordinate and angular observations. Mathematics is then used to summarise the data, estimate reliability, model uncertainty, resolve coordinates and solve adjustment equations.

Learning Outcome 1: Apply statistics and probability Measures of central tendency, standard deviation, grouped and ungrouped data, and probability distributions.
Learning Outcome 2: Apply vector theory Vector and scalar quantities, vector algebra, dot and cross products, gradient, divergence and curl.
Learning Outcome 3: Apply matrices Determinants, inverses of 2×2 and 3×3 matrices, and solution of simultaneous equations.

Survey calculation discipline: define the observed quantities, write the correct formula, substitute values carefully, compute with appropriate precision, attach units and interpret the result in a surveying context.

Learning Outcome 1

1. Apply Statistics and Probability

Statistics helps surveyors summarise repeated observations and judge their consistency. Probability helps surveyors reason about uncertainty, error occurrence, tolerance limits and expected behaviour of survey measurements.

Key survey idea

No field observation is perfectly exact. A measured distance, bearing or level is affected by instrument limitations, environmental conditions, observer judgement and random variation. Statistical methods provide a disciplined way to summarise and evaluate these observations.

Survey Situation Mathematical Tool Purpose
Repeated distance measurements Mean and standard deviation Estimate best value and consistency
Levelling readings from different setups Grouped / ungrouped data Organise and compare observations
Readings outside tolerance Probability Estimate likelihood of unacceptable readings
Counting rare blunders per survey job Poisson distribution Model rare event counts
Measurement errors around a mean Normal distribution Model random errors and confidence behaviour
1.1.1 Mean

Mean of Survey Observations

The mean is the arithmetic average. In land survey, repeated readings are often averaged to reduce the effect of random error.

Ungrouped Mean

\[\bar{x}=\frac{\sum x}{n}\]

Where $\bar{x}$ is the mean, $\sum x$ is the sum of observations and $n$ is the number of observations.

Worked Example 1: Mean Distance

A survey line is measured four times as $125.42\text{ m}$, $125.39\text{ m}$, $125.44\text{ m}$ and $125.41\text{ m}$. Find the mean distance.

  1. Formula: \[\bar{x}=\frac{\sum x}{n}\]
  2. Substitute: \[\bar{x}=\frac{125.42+125.39+125.44+125.41}{4}\]
  3. \[\bar{x}=\frac{501.66}{4}=125.415\text{ m}\]
  4. Answer: The mean distance is $125.415\text{ m}$, approximately $125.42\text{ m}$ to two decimal places.

Worked Example 2: Mean Level Reading

Staff readings on a benchmark are $1.246\text{ m}$, $1.249\text{ m}$, $1.245\text{ m}$ and $1.248\text{ m}$. Find the mean reading.

  1. \[\bar{x}=\frac{1.246+1.249+1.245+1.248}{4}\]
  2. \[\bar{x}=\frac{4.988}{4}=1.247\text{ m}\]
  3. Survey interpretation: The best representative staff reading is $1.247\text{ m}$.
1.1.2 Median

Median

The median is the middle value when observations are arranged in order. It is less affected by extreme values than the mean.

Median Position

For odd $n$: \[\text{Median}=\left(\frac{n+1}{2}\right)^{\text{th}}\text{ value}\]
For even $n$: \[\text{Median}=\frac{\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)^{\text{th}}+\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)^{\text{th}}}{2}\]

Worked Example 3: Median of Angular Readings

Repeated angular readings in degrees are $64.22$, $64.18$, $64.25$, $64.20$, $64.21$. Find the median.

  1. Arrange in ascending order: \[64.18,\ 64.20,\ 64.21,\ 64.22,\ 64.25\]
  2. There are five observations, so the median is the third value.
  3. Answer: Median angle $=64.21^\circ$.

Worked Example 4: Median with Even Number of Observations

Measured offsets are $2.4\text{ m}$, $2.8\text{ m}$, $2.6\text{ m}$ and $3.0\text{ m}$. Find the median.

  1. Arrange: \[2.4,\ 2.6,\ 2.8,\ 3.0\]
  2. Middle two values are $2.6$ and $2.8$.
  3. \[\text{Median}=\frac{2.6+2.8}{2}=2.7\text{ m}\]
1.1.3 Mode

Mode

The mode is the most frequently occurring value. It can indicate the most common reading, error class, defect count or equipment response.

Important note

A data set may have one mode, more than one mode or no mode. In continuous survey measurements, exact repeated values may be rare, so modal class is often more useful than exact mode.

Worked Example 5: Mode of Repeated Readings

Staff readings are $1.235$, $1.236$, $1.235$, $1.237$, $1.235$, $1.236$. Find the mode.

  1. $1.235$ occurs three times.
  2. $1.236$ occurs two times.
  3. $1.237$ occurs once.
  4. Answer: Mode $=1.235\text{ m}$.
1.1.4 Standard Deviation

Standard Deviation

Standard deviation measures how spread out the observations are from the mean. A small standard deviation suggests consistent observations; a large standard deviation suggests poor precision or possible blunders.

Standard Deviation Formulae

Population standard deviation: \[\sigma=\sqrt{\frac{\sum(x-\mu)^2}{N}}\]
Sample standard deviation: \[s=\sqrt{\frac{\sum(x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1}}\]
Variance: \[s^2=\frac{\sum(x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1}\]
Standard error of mean: \[SE=\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\]

Worked Example 6: Sample Standard Deviation of Distance Readings

Distance readings are $50.02\text{ m}$, $50.04\text{ m}$, $50.01\text{ m}$ and $50.03\text{ m}$. Find the sample standard deviation.

  1. Mean: \[\bar{x}=\frac{50.02+50.04+50.01+50.03}{4}=50.025\text{ m}\]
  2. Deviations: \[-0.005,\ 0.015,\ -0.015,\ 0.005\]
  3. Squares: \[0.000025,\ 0.000225,\ 0.000225,\ 0.000025\]
  4. Sum of squares: \[\sum(x-\bar{x})^2=0.0005\]
  5. \[s=\sqrt{\frac{0.0005}{4-1}}=\sqrt{0.0001667}=0.0129\text{ m}\]
  6. Answer: Standard deviation $\approx0.013\text{ m}$.

Survey interpretation

The mean gives the central value, but standard deviation tells whether the observations are reliable. Two data sets can have the same mean but very different consistency.

1.2 Grouped and ungrouped data

Grouped and Ungrouped Data

Ungrouped data consists of raw individual observations. Grouped data is organised into classes. Grouping is useful when many observations are collected, such as staff readings, offsets, chainage errors or repeated coordinate residuals.

Ungrouped data

Example: repeated staff readings

\[1.245,\ 1.247,\ 1.246,\ 1.250,\ 1.248\]

Grouped data

Example: distance error classes

\[0-5\text{ mm},\ 6-10\text{ mm},\ 11-15\text{ mm}\]

Grouped Mean

\[\bar{x}=\frac{\sum fx}{\sum f}\]

Where $f$ is frequency and $x$ is the class midpoint.

Worked Example 7: Grouped Mean of Residual Errors

The table shows residual error classes from a survey adjustment.

Error Class (mm) Frequency $f$ Midpoint $x$ $fx$
0 - 4326
5 - 95735
10 - 1441248
15 - 1921734
  1. \[\sum f=3+5+4+2=14\]
  2. \[\sum fx=6+35+48+34=123\]
  3. \[\bar{x}=\frac{123}{14}=8.79\text{ mm}\]
  4. Answer: The estimated mean residual error is $8.79\text{ mm}$.
1.3 Probability

Probability Concepts for Survey Decisions

Probability measures likelihood. In surveying, probability supports reasoning about repeated observations, tolerance failure, occurrence of blunders and uncertainty of measured values.

Basic Probability Formulae

\[P(A)=\frac{n(A)}{n(S)}\]
\[0\leq P(A)\leq1\]
\[P(A')=1-P(A)\]
\[P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)\]

Worked Example 8: Probability of Out-of-Tolerance Reading

In 40 repeated readings, 6 are outside tolerance. Find the probability that a randomly selected reading is outside tolerance.

  1. \[P(A)=\frac{n(A)}{n(S)}\]
  2. \[P(\text{outside tolerance})=\frac{6}{40}=0.15\]
  3. Answer: Probability $=0.15$ or $15\%$.
1.3.1 Binomial distribution

Binomial Distribution

The binomial distribution applies where there is a fixed number of independent trials, each trial has two outcomes, and the probability of success remains constant.

Survey use case

A reading may be classified as either within tolerance or outside tolerance. If the probability of an out-of-tolerance reading is known, the binomial distribution can estimate the probability of getting a certain number of unacceptable readings in a fixed sample.

Binomial Probability

\[P(X=x)=\binom{n}{x}p^xq^{n-x}\]
\[q=1-p\]
\[\binom{n}{x}=\frac{n!}{x!(n-x)!}\]
Mean: \[\mu=np\]
Variance: \[\sigma^2=npq\]

Worked Example 9: Binomial Survey Tolerance Problem

The probability that a distance reading is outside tolerance is $0.10$. In 5 readings, find the probability that exactly 1 reading is outside tolerance.

  1. Here $n=5$, $x=1$, $p=0.10$, $q=0.90$.
  2. \[P(X=1)=\binom{5}{1}(0.10)^1(0.90)^4\]
  3. \[P(X=1)=5(0.10)(0.6561)=0.32805\]
  4. Answer: Probability $=0.3281$ or $32.81\%$.

Worked Example 10: At Least One Unacceptable Reading

If $p=0.05$ for an unacceptable staff reading, find the probability of at least one unacceptable reading in 4 readings.

  1. Use complement: \[P(X\geq1)=1-P(X=0)\]
  2. \[P(X=0)=\binom{4}{0}(0.05)^0(0.95)^4\]
  3. \[P(X=0)=0.8145\]
  4. \[P(X\geq1)=1-0.8145=0.1855\]
  5. Answer: Probability $=18.55\%$.
1.3.2 Poisson distribution

Poisson Distribution

The Poisson distribution models the number of times an event occurs in a fixed interval of time, area, length or survey job when the event is relatively rare and occurs independently.

Survey use case

Poisson distribution can model rare blunders per traverse, instrument faults per month, missing survey marks per route section, or number of readings exceeding tolerance in a fixed observation period.

Poisson Probability

\[P(X=x)=\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x}{x!}\]
Mean: \[\mu=\lambda\]
Variance: \[\sigma^2=\lambda\]
Standard deviation: \[\sigma=\sqrt{\lambda}\]
\[e\approx2.71828\]

Worked Example 11: Rare Blunders in a Survey Job

A survey team records an average of $2$ blunders per large survey job. Find the probability of exactly $3$ blunders in one job.

  1. Here $\lambda=2$ and $x=3$.
  2. \[P(X=3)=\frac{e^{-2}2^3}{3!}\]
  3. \[P(X=3)=\frac{0.1353\times8}{6}=0.1804\]
  4. Answer: Probability $\approx0.1804$ or $18.04\%$.

Worked Example 12: Probability of No Instrument Fault

An instrument develops on average $0.5$ faults per month. Find the probability of no fault in a month.

  1. Here $\lambda=0.5$ and $x=0$.
  2. \[P(X=0)=\frac{e^{-0.5}(0.5)^0}{0!}\]
  3. \[P(X=0)=e^{-0.5}=0.6065\]
  4. Answer: Probability $\approx60.65\%$.
1.3.3 Normal distribution

Normal Distribution

The normal distribution is a continuous bell-shaped distribution. Many random measurement errors are approximately normal because they arise from many small independent influences.

Normal Distribution

\[X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\]
Standard score: \[z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\]
Standard normal: \[Z\sim N(0,1)\]
Approx. rule: \[68\%\text{ within }1\sigma\]
Approx. rule: \[95\%\text{ within }2\sigma\]

Worked Example 13: Standard Score of a Distance Error

Distance errors are normally distributed with mean $0\text{ mm}$ and standard deviation $5\text{ mm}$. Find the z-score for an error of $8\text{ mm}$.

  1. Formula: \[z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\]
  2. Substitute: \[z=\frac{8-0}{5}=1.6\]
  3. Answer: The error is $1.6$ standard deviations above the mean.

Worked Example 14: Interpreting the 95% Rule

Repeated levelling errors have mean $0\text{ mm}$ and standard deviation $3\text{ mm}$. Estimate the range containing about $95\%$ of errors.

  1. About $95\%$ of values lie within $2\sigma$ of the mean.
  2. \[2\sigma=2(3)=6\text{ mm}\]
  3. Range: \[0\pm6\text{ mm}\]
  4. Answer: About $95\%$ of errors lie between $-6\text{ mm}$ and $+6\text{ mm}$.
Learning Outcome 2

2. Apply Vector Theory

Vector theory is central to land survey because position, displacement, bearing, force direction, slope direction and coordinate changes all have magnitude and direction. Vectors also support coordinate geometry, traversing and spatial modelling.

2.1 Vectors and scalar quantities

Scalars and Vectors

A scalar has magnitude only. A vector has both magnitude and direction.

Quantity Type Meaning Survey Examples
Scalar Magnitude only Distance, area, volume, temperature, time, mass
Vector Magnitude and direction Displacement, velocity, force, coordinate shift, direction of gradient

Vector Representation

2D vector: \[\mathbf{a}=a_x\mathbf{i}+a_y\mathbf{j}\]
3D vector: \[\mathbf{a}=a_x\mathbf{i}+a_y\mathbf{j}+a_z\mathbf{k}\]
Magnitude in 2D: \[|\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{a_x^2+a_y^2}\]
Magnitude in 3D: \[|\mathbf{a}|=\sqrt{a_x^2+a_y^2+a_z^2}\]

Worked Example 15: Magnitude of a Coordinate Displacement

A point moves from $A(100,200)$ to $B(106,208)$. Find the displacement vector and its magnitude.

  1. Displacement vector: \[\mathbf{AB}=(106-100)\mathbf{i}+(208-200)\mathbf{j}\]
  2. \[\mathbf{AB}=6\mathbf{i}+8\mathbf{j}\]
  3. Magnitude: \[|\mathbf{AB}|=\sqrt{6^2+8^2}=\sqrt{100}=10\]
  4. Answer: The displacement is $6\mathbf{i}+8\mathbf{j}$ and the distance is $10\text{ units}$.
2.2 Vector algebra

Vector Algebra

Vector algebra involves addition, subtraction and scalar multiplication. It is used to combine coordinate shifts, resolve traverse components and represent movement from one station to another.

Vector Operations

Addition: \[\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}=(a_x+b_x)\mathbf{i}+(a_y+b_y)\mathbf{j}\]
Subtraction: \[\mathbf{a}-\mathbf{b}=(a_x-b_x)\mathbf{i}+(a_y-b_y)\mathbf{j}\]
Scalar multiplication: \[k\mathbf{a}=ka_x\mathbf{i}+ka_y\mathbf{j}\]
Unit vector: \[\hat{\mathbf{a}}=\frac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|}\]
