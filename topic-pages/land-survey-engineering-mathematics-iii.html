Engineering Mathematics III for Land Survey

Statistics, probability, matrices, errors and adjustment concepts for higher land survey mathematics.

Professional learner notes

Purpose of the topic

Engineering Mathematics III supports survey-data analysis, repeated observations, matrix correction equations and interpretation of measurement error.

These notes are written as class notes for engineering trainees. They follow the same working standard used in class: state the formula, substitute correctly, compute, attach units, and then explain what the answer means in a civil, building or survey situation.

Section 1

Survey data and repeated observations

Survey measurements are repeated to reduce blunders and estimate reliability.

Key formulae

\[\bar{x}=\frac{\sum x}{n}\]

Worked example

Angles 45°10′, 45°12′ and 45°11′ average to 45°11′.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Mean observations reduce random variation.

Section 2

Dispersion and standard deviation

Dispersion shows how spread out observations are.

Key formulae

\[s=\sqrt{\frac{\sum(x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1}}\]

Worked example

Small standard deviation means observations are consistent.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used to judge reliability of repeated readings.

Section 3

Probability in quality decisions

Probability estimates likelihood of acceptable or unacceptable observations.

Key formulae

\[P(A)=\frac{n(A)}{n(S)}\]

Worked example

If 2 out of 20 readings are outside tolerance, sample probability=0.10.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Supports quality-control judgement.

Section 4

Matrices for survey adjustment

Adjustment equations can be organised in matrix form.

Key formulae

\[AX=B\]

Worked example

Correction equations for unknowns can be arranged in a coefficient matrix.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Matrix methods support traverse and network adjustment.

Section 5

Error interpretation

Error is difference between observed and accepted value.

Key formulae

\[e=\mathrm{observed}-\mathrm{true}\]

Worked example

If observed distance is 100.026 m and accepted value is 100.000 m, error=0.026 m.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Understanding error is central to survey accuracy.

Practice exercises

  1. Find mean of 5 repeated distance observations.
  2. Explain random and systematic error.
  3. Find probability if 3 of 30 readings are outside tolerance.
  4. Write two correction equations as AX=B.
  5. Calculate error for observed 75.014 m and accepted 75.000 m.

Summary

By the end of this topic, a competent trainee should be able to identify the correct formula, perform the calculation accurately, use correct units, and explain the result in a real engineering context.
