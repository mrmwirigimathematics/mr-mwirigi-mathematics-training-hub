Learner notes

Topic Overview

Building work involves slopes, roof pitch, ramps, staircases, diagonals, setting out, levels and graphical interpretation of site data. Trigonometry, geometry and linear graphs provide the mathematical tools for handling these tasks accurately.

Learning Outcome 1: Perform trigonometry calculations Trigonometric ratios, right-angle triangle calculations, angles of elevation and depression, slope and roof pitch.
Learning Outcome 2: Perform geometric calculations Geometrical figures, areas of figures and application of Pythagoras' theorem in building layouts.
Learning Outcome 3: Apply linear graphs Data sets, graph scales, axes labelling, plotting straight-line graphs and analysing real site graphs.

Professional working rule: draw a clear sketch, label known values, identify the unknown, choose the correct formula, substitute carefully, then state the answer with correct units.

Learning Outcome 1

1. Perform Trigonometry Calculations

Trigonometry deals with relationships between angles and sides of triangles. In building, it is used in roof slopes, stairs, ramps, bracing, setting out and height estimation.

1.1 Trigonometric Ratios

In a right-angled triangle, the three main sides are the hypotenuse, opposite side and adjacent side.

Right-Angled Triangle Labelling

The side opposite the right angle is always the hypotenuse. The side opposite the angle $\theta$ is the opposite side. The side next to $\theta$, but not the hypotenuse, is the adjacent side.

Remember the ratios using SOH-CAH-TOA.

Main Trigonometric Ratios

\[\sin\theta=\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\]
\[\cos\theta=\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\]
\[\tan\theta=\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}\]
\[\theta=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}\right)\]

1.2 Choosing the Correct Ratio

Known / Required Sides Ratio to Use Building Example
Opposite and hypotenuse $\sin\theta$ Finding height reached by an inclined ladder
Adjacent and hypotenuse $\cos\theta$ Finding horizontal projection of a roof member
Opposite and adjacent $\tan\theta$ Finding roof pitch, ramp angle or gradient

Worked Example 1: Finding the Height of a Ladder

A ladder of length $6\text{ m}$ leans against a wall at an angle of $65^\circ$ to the ground. Find the vertical height reached.

  1. The ladder is the hypotenuse. The required height is the opposite side.
  2. Use sine: \[\sin\theta=\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\]
  3. Substitute: \[\sin65^\circ=\frac{h}{6}\]
  4. Make $h$ the subject: \[h=6\sin65^\circ\]
  5. Calculate: \[h=6(0.9063)=5.44\text{ m}\]
  6. Answer: The ladder reaches approximately $5.44\text{ m}$ vertically.

Worked Example 2: Finding Roof Pitch Angle

A roof rises $1.8\text{ m}$ over a horizontal span of $4.5\text{ m}$. Find the pitch angle.

  1. The rise is the opposite side and the horizontal span is the adjacent side.
  2. Use tangent: \[\tan\theta=\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}\]
  3. Substitute: \[\tan\theta=\frac{1.8}{4.5}=0.4\]
  4. Find the angle: \[\theta=\tan^{-1}(0.4)=21.8^\circ\]
  5. Answer: The roof pitch angle is approximately $21.8^\circ$.

Worked Example 3: Finding Ramp Length

A ramp rises $0.75\text{ m}$ at an angle of $10^\circ$ to the horizontal. Find the ramp length.

  1. The rise is the opposite side. The ramp length is the hypotenuse.
  2. Use sine: \[\sin10^\circ=\frac{0.75}{L}\]
  3. Make $L$ the subject: \[L=\frac{0.75}{\sin10^\circ}\]
  4. Calculate: \[L=\frac{0.75}{0.1736}=4.32\text{ m}\]
  5. Answer: The ramp length should be approximately $4.32\text{ m}$.

Calculator caution

Ensure the calculator is in degree mode when working with angles measured in degrees. If the calculator is in radian mode, trigonometric answers will be wrong.

Learning Outcome 2

2. Perform Geometric Calculations

Geometry is the study of shapes, sizes and positions. Building students apply geometry when calculating areas, setting out rectangular corners, checking diagonals and interpreting drawings.

2.1 Geometrical Figures

Figure Key Properties Building Application
Triangle Three sides and three angles Roof trusses, bracing, gable ends
Rectangle Opposite sides equal, four right angles Rooms, slabs, walls, windows and doors
Square Four equal sides and four right angles Tiles, columns, paving units
Circle All points are equally distant from the centre Manholes, circular columns, tanks and pipes
Trapezium One pair of parallel sides Irregular wall panels, drainage sections, embankments

2.2 Areas of Figures

Area measures surface coverage. Building applications include floor finishes, roof sheets, wall plastering, painting, formwork and landscaping.

Area Formulae

Rectangle: \[A=lw\]
Square: \[A=s^2\]
Triangle: \[A=\frac{1}{2}bh\]
Parallelogram: \[A=bh\]
Trapezium: \[A=\frac{1}{2}(a+b)h\]
Circle: \[A=\pi r^2\]

Worked Example 4: Area of a Gable End

A triangular gable end has base $8\text{ m}$ and height $2.4\text{ m}$. Find its area.

  1. Use triangle area: \[A=\frac{1}{2}bh\]
  2. Substitute: \[A=\frac{1}{2}\times8\times2.4\]
  3. Calculate: \[A=9.6\text{ m}^2\]
  4. Answer: The gable end area is $9.6\text{ m}^2$.

Worked Example 5: Area of a Circular Manhole Cover

A circular manhole cover has radius $0.35\text{ m}$. Find its area.

  1. Use circle area: \[A=\pi r^2\]
  2. Substitute: \[A=\pi(0.35)^2\]
  3. Calculate: \[A=0.3848\text{ m}^2\]
  4. Answer: The area is approximately $0.385\text{ m}^2$.

2.3 Apply Pythagoras' Theorem

Pythagoras' theorem applies only to right-angled triangles. It is used to find missing sides and check whether corners are square during setting out.

Pythagoras' Theorem

The hypotenuse is the longest side and is opposite the right angle.

\[c^2=a^2+b^2\]

If finding one shorter side:

\[a=\sqrt{c^2-b^2}\]

Worked Example 6: Checking the Diagonal of a Room

A rectangular room is $6\text{ m}$ long and $4\text{ m}$ wide. Find the diagonal length.

  1. Use Pythagoras: \[c^2=a^2+b^2\]
  2. Substitute: \[c^2=6^2+4^2\]
  3. \[c^2=36+16=52\]
  4. \[c=\sqrt{52}=7.21\text{ m}\]
  5. Answer: The diagonal is approximately $7.21\text{ m}$.

Worked Example 7: The 3-4-5 Method for Setting Out

A builder wants to check a right angle using sides $3\text{ m}$ and $4\text{ m}$. What should the diagonal be?

  1. Use Pythagoras: \[c^2=3^2+4^2\]
  2. \[c^2=9+16=25\]
  3. \[c=\sqrt{25}=5\text{ m}\]
  4. Interpretation: If the diagonal is $5\text{ m}$, the corner is square.
Learning Outcome 3

3. Apply Linear Graphs

A linear graph is a straight-line graph. It shows a relationship where one quantity changes at a constant rate with another quantity. In building, linear graphs can represent cost, distance, temperature, velocity and production trends.

3.1 Data Set Identification

A data set is a collection of values. Before plotting a graph, identify the independent variable and dependent variable.

Graph Type Independent Variable Dependent Variable Meaning
Distance against time Time Distance Distance depends on time travelled
Temperature against time Time Temperature Temperature changes as time passes
Velocity against time Time Velocity Velocity depends on time

3.2 Scales in Graphs

A scale shows how much each square or division on an axis represents. A good scale must fit the data clearly and use convenient intervals such as 1, 2, 5, 10, 20 or 50.

Graph scale rule

Do not choose a scale that compresses the graph into a small corner. The plotted graph should occupy most of the graph area.

3.3 Axes Labelling

Axes must be labelled with the quantity and the unit. For example, write Time (s), not just Time.

Axis Usually Represents Example Label
x-axis Independent variable Time (min)
y-axis Dependent variable Distance (m)

3.4 Linear Graph Plotting

To plot a linear graph, draw axes, choose a suitable scale, label the axes, plot points accurately and join them with a straight line.

Straight Line Equation and Gradient

\[y=mx+c\]
\[m=\frac{\text{change in }y}{\text{change in }x}=\frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}\]
\[c=\text{y-intercept}\]
\[\text{Straight line means constant gradient}\]

Worked Example 8: Plotting a Linear Cost Graph

A delivery service charges a fixed fee of KSh 500 plus KSh 200 per trip. The cost equation is:

\[C=200n+500\]

Find the cost for $n=0,1,2,3,4$ trips.

Trips $n$ 0 1 2 3 4
Cost $C$ 500 700 900 1100 1300
  1. Plot the points $(0,500)$, $(1,700)$, $(2,900)$, $(3,1100)$ and $(4,1300)$.
  2. Join the points using a straight line.
  3. The gradient is $200$, meaning cost increases by KSh 200 per trip.
  4. The intercept is $500$, meaning the fixed charge is KSh 500.
Graph applications

4. Graph Applications in Building and Engineering

4.1 Distance Against Time

A distance-time graph shows how far an object or worker moves with time. The gradient represents speed.

\[\text{Speed}=\frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}\]

Worked Example 9: Speed from a Distance-Time Graph

A site vehicle moves $120\text{ m}$ in $40\text{ s}$. Find the speed.

  1. Use speed formula: \[v=\frac{d}{t}\]
  2. Substitute: \[v=\frac{120}{40}=3\text{ m/s}\]
  3. Answer: The speed is $3\text{ m/s}$.

4.2 Temperature Against Time

A temperature-time graph can be used when monitoring curing conditions, heating, cooling or environmental site conditions.

Worked Example 10: Temperature Change Rate

Concrete curing temperature changes from $20^\circ\text{C}$ to $32^\circ\text{C}$ in 6 hours. Find the average rate of change.

  1. Temperature change: \[32-20=12^\circ\text{C}\]
  2. Rate of change: \[\frac{12}{6}=2^\circ\text{C/h}\]
  3. Answer: The average temperature increase is $2^\circ\text{C}$ per hour.

4.3 Velocity Against Time

A velocity-time graph shows motion. The gradient represents acceleration, while the area under the graph represents distance travelled.

Velocity-Time Graph Formulae

\[\text{Acceleration}=\frac{\text{change in velocity}}{\text{time taken}}\]
\[\text{Distance}=\text{area under velocity-time graph}\]
For rectangle area: \[A=bh\]
For triangle area: \[A=\frac{1}{2}bh\]

Worked Example 11: Acceleration from Velocity-Time Data

A hoist changes velocity from $0\text{ m/s}$ to $4\text{ m/s}$ in $8\text{ s}$. Find the acceleration.

  1. Use acceleration formula: \[a=\frac{v-u}{t}\]
  2. Substitute: \[a=\frac{4-0}{8}=0.5\text{ m/s}^2\]
  3. Answer: The acceleration is $0.5\text{ m/s}^2$.

4.4 Graph Analysis

Graph analysis means explaining what the graph shows. A professional interpretation should mention the variables, units, trend, gradient, intercept, maximum and minimum values, and the practical meaning of the graph.

Graph analysis checklist

  1. Read the title of the graph.
  2. Identify the x-axis and y-axis quantities.
  3. Check the units and scale.
  4. Identify the highest and lowest values.
  5. Determine whether the graph is increasing, decreasing or constant.
  6. Calculate gradient where required.
  7. Interpret the result in the building or engineering context.

Practice Questions for Building Students

  1. A ladder $5\text{ m}$ long leans against a wall at an angle of $70^\circ$ to the ground. Find the vertical height reached.
  2. A ramp rises $0.6\text{ m}$ over a horizontal distance of $5\text{ m}$. Find the angle of inclination.
  3. A roof rises $2.1\text{ m}$ over a horizontal span of $6\text{ m}$. Calculate the pitch angle.
  4. Find the area of a rectangular floor measuring $7.5\text{ m}$ by $4.2\text{ m}$.
  5. Find the area of a triangular gable with base $10\text{ m}$ and height $3\text{ m}$.
  6. A rectangular room measures $8\text{ m}$ by $6\text{ m}$. Use Pythagoras' theorem to find the diagonal.
  7. A right-angled triangle has hypotenuse $13\text{ m}$ and one shorter side $5\text{ m}$. Find the other shorter side.
  8. Plot the graph of $y=2x+3$ for $x=0,1,2,3,4$.
  9. A site vehicle travels $150\text{ m}$ in $50\text{ s}$. Find the speed.
  10. Temperature changes from $18^\circ\text{C}$ to $30^\circ\text{C}$ in 4 hours. Find the average rate of temperature change.
  11. A hoist increases velocity from $1\text{ m/s}$ to $5\text{ m/s}$ in $8\text{ s}$. Find the acceleration.
  12. State four rules for drawing a professional linear graph.
Competency checklist

Summary Checklist

A competent learner can:

  • Identify opposite, adjacent and hypotenuse sides.
  • Apply sine, cosine and tangent ratios correctly.
  • Use inverse trigonometry to find angles.
  • Calculate areas of common geometrical figures.
  • Apply Pythagoras' theorem to building layouts.
  • Identify independent and dependent variables.
  • Select suitable graph scales and label axes correctly.
  • Plot and analyse linear graphs.
  • Interpret distance-time, temperature-time and velocity-time graphs.

Common mistakes to avoid:

  • Using trigonometry without drawing and labelling a triangle.
  • Confusing opposite and adjacent sides.
  • Using calculator radian mode instead of degree mode.
  • Applying Pythagoras' theorem to a triangle that is not right-angled.
  • Writing area answers without square units.
  • Choosing poor graph scales.
  • Forgetting units on graph axes.
  • Describing graph trends without explaining their practical meaning.
