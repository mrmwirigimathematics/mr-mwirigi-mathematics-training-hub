Basic Mathematics II for Building Technology

Geometry, mensuration, graphs and statistics for building measurement and construction work.

Professional learner notes

Purpose of the topic

Basic Mathematics II expands measurement work into areas, volumes, graphs and interpretation of building data.

These notes are written as class notes for engineering trainees. They follow the same working standard used in class: state the formula, substitute correctly, compute, attach units, and then explain what the answer means in a civil, building or survey situation.

Section 1

Plane geometry

Building drawings use rectangles, triangles, circles and trapezia.

Key formulae

\[A=lw\]
\[A=\frac12 bh\]
\[A=\pi r^2\]

Worked example

A triangular gable with base 6 m and height 2 m has area 6 m².

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used in wall and roof-face area calculations.

Section 2

Volume calculations

Concrete, mortar, excavation and storage require volume calculations.

Key formulae

\[V=lwh\]
\[V=\pi r^2h\]

Worked example

Slab 8 m by 5 m by 0.15 m has volume 6 m³.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used for ordering concrete.

Section 3

Surface area

Surface area supports plastering, painting and cladding estimates.

Key formulae

\[\mathrm{Wall\ area}=lh\]

Worked example

A wall 12 m long and 3 m high has area 36 m² before openings.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Used in finish quantities.

Section 4

Graphs

Graphs show cost, production and time relationships.

Key formulae

\[y=mx+c\]

Worked example

C=500x+2000 has fixed cost 2000 and rate 500 per unit.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Useful in estimating and productivity.

Section 5

Grouped and ungrouped data

Site data can be organised in frequency tables and charts.

Key formulae

\[\bar{x}=\frac{\sum x}{n}\]

Worked example

Mean can summarise daily block production.

Method discipline: write the formula first, substitute known values, simplify carefully, attach correct units, and interpret the answer.

Engineering interpretation

Supports site supervision.

Practice exercises

  1. Find wall area after subtracting a 2 m² window.
  2. Find cylinder volume for r=0.4 m and h=1.5 m.
  3. Interpret C=750x+3000.
  4. Draw a table for block outputs over 5 days.
  5. Calculate mean of 12,14,13,15,16.

Summary

By the end of this topic, a competent trainee should be able to identify the correct formula, perform the calculation accurately, use correct units, and explain the result in a real engineering context.
